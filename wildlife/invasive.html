Cheatgrass Observations

Low

High



Concentration of Cheatgrass observations from 2005 to present. Areas with no observations do not necessarily lack species occurances.


Quick Facts:
⚬ Native to Europe.

⚬ Introduced for dune control and as a highway ornamental.

⚬ Costly to contain due to prolific seed production (up to 10,000 a year).

⚬ Estimated annual costs to contain Scotch Broom in Oregon exceed $47 million.

Quick Facts:
⚬ Native to Europe and Asia.

⚬ Probably introduced to Canada as a crop contaminant.

⚬ Grows in circular patches and replaces native plants as it spreads.

⚬ Can result in crop reduction up to 25%.


Quick Facts:
⚬ Native to Europe and Asia.

⚬ Probably introduced as a contaminant in imported grain.

⚬ Disrupts the health of pastures and rangeland.

⚬ Secretes a milky sap that is irritating to human skin, and may cause hair loss on horses.


Quick Facts:
⚬ Native to Europe and Eastern Asia.

⚬ Probably an escaped ornamental.

⚬ Primarily invades rangeland and pine woodland, seriously diminishing native crop production.


Quick Facts:
⚬ Native to Europe.

⚬ Probably introduced as a contaminant in seeds and livestock.

⚬ Forms dense strands, completely excluding native plants.

⚬ Costs for control often exceed the potential economic benefits from grazing.


Quick Facts:
⚬ Native to Europe.

⚬ Originally cultivated for food and medicine.

⚬ Displaces vegetation in the forest understory, which decreases forage availability for deer.


Quick Facts:
⚬ Native to the Himalayas.

⚬ Introduced for berry production.

⚬ One of the most economically disruptive of all noxious weeds in the PNW.

⚬ Displaces native plant species and dominates many riparian habitats.


Quick Facts:
⚬ Native to Europe and Asia.

⚬ Introduced as a contaminant in imported grain and straw packing material.

⚬ Can quickly overwhelm native plants.

⚬ Cheatgrass strands dry out earlier than most native plants, increasing risk for an earlier fire season.

Invasive Crayfish Observations

Low

High



Concentration of invasive Crayfish observations from 2005 to present. Areas with no observations do not necessarily lack species occurances.

Quick Facts:
⚬ Native to South America.

⚬ Introduced for fur farming in the early 1900s.

⚬ Construct burrows in wetlands, which can cause considerable erosion.

⚬ They have nearly eliminated populations of native muskrat.

Quick Facts:
⚬ Native to the Eastern United States.

⚬ Introduced as a food item.

⚬ Can lay up to 20,000 eggs annually, often 2-4 times as much as native frog species.

⚬ Aggressive predators, known to decimate populations of native turtles and frogs.

Quick Facts:
⚬ Native to Europe.

⚬ First introduced to New York in 1890, and spread across the continental United States.

⚬ Thrive in urban areas, where they are known to outcompete native songbirds.

Quick Facts:
⚬ There are four invasive crayfish species in the Pacific Northwest: Rusty Crayfish, Red Swamp Crayfish, Ringed Crayfish, and Virile Crayfish.

⚬ All invasive crayfish present are native to the Eastern United States.

⚬ Compete with the native Signal Crayfish.

⚬ Specific introduction paths are unknown, but it is likely that invasive crayfish were introduced as live bait or as released pets.
